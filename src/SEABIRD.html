Common Murre

The Common Murre (Uria aalge) is a seabird in the family Alcidae, found in the northern hemisphere. It is known for its striking appearance and its remarkable diving and swimming capabilities.

Physical Characteristics

The Common Murre has a black head, neck, and back, with white underparts. It has a slender, pointed beak and a long, straight bill, adapted for catching fish. Its wings are short and pointed, aiding in its impressive underwater swimming.

Habitat

Common Murres are typically found along rocky coasts and cliff edges in the northern Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. They nest in large colonies on steep cliffs, where they lay their eggs on bare rock ledges.

Behavior and Diet

This seabird is a skilled diver, capable of diving to depths of up to 100 meters (328 feet) in search of fish. Common Murres primarily feed on small fish, such as herring and anchovies, and sometimes consume crustaceans and other marine organisms.

Reproduction

The Common Murre breeds in large colonies during the summer months. They lay a single egg on a rocky ledge, which both parents incubate. After hatching, the chick is fed by regurgitation until it is ready to fledge and dive into the sea.

Conservation Status

While the Common Murre is currently not listed as endangered, it faces threats from climate change, oil spills, and bycatch in fisheries. Conservation efforts focus on protecting their breeding sites and managing fishery impacts to ensure their populations remain stable.
